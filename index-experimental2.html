The Digital Oral History and Transcript Analysis Platform is designed to present and analyze a selection of oral history interviews through an intuitive and visually engaging website. With a layout modeled on a clean interactive research environment, the site hosts readable, annotated transcripts and audio files enhanced by modern natural language processing tools. These include keyword frequency tracking, and named entity recognition, helping users explore themes, patterns, and significant terms that emerge organically from the interview content.

This project leverages general corpus linguistic methods alongside manual interpretation to bridge the gap between machine analysis and human understanding. Through integration with transcription tools such as OpenAI Whisper and PDF parsing capabilities, the platform automates large portions of data processing while offering editorial oversight. The goal is not only to present archival content but also to interpret it—highlighting historical voices and surfacing previously obscured narratives through interactive visualizations and data-driven commentary.

The platform serves as both a research tool and public history resource. It embeds metadata, interview context, and networked relationships into the digital space, offering insights into the lives and legacies of the interviewees. The user is encouraged to explore both the technical infrastructure of the transcripts and the thematic core of the project, using linked visual tools, graphs, and text analysis widgets to build their own interpretations of the oral history materials.

Legal Network Themes Across Civil Rights Leaders

1. Shared Professional and Institutional Anchoring

The prominence of tightly connected terms such as law, court, case, judge, constitution, and justice reveals that all three speakers are deeply embedded in the legal domain. These aren't just frequent words—they are central nodes with strong ties to multiple other terms, which indicates a structural role in the discourse.

This suggests that each speaker not only worked within the legal system but consistently framed their experiences through legal concepts. This is consistent with their professional identities as lawyers and judges involved in civil rights litigation.

2. Legal Advocacy and Systemic Reform

Words like rights, segregation, movement, and race appearing with strong co-occurrence ties to legal terminology reflect that the legal fight for civil rights is a dominant topic. These aren’t discussed in isolation but are embedded in institutional contexts—showing that these men were not simply observers but active strategists in legal reform.

For example:

  • The word segregation is likely tied to education and case, suggesting frequent discussions of landmark legal efforts such as Brown v. Board of Education.
  • The connection between justice and community highlights how they linked systemic legal change with broader societal transformation.

3. Education as a Strategic Battleground

The presence of school, education, university, and Carolina (likely referring to South Carolina where Perry practiced) indicates that educational institutions were a focal point of struggle and strategy.

This aligns with known history: legal battles over desegregation often centered on public schools and universities, and these speakers played direct roles in those transformations. Their discourse doesn’t treat education as abstract—it’s intertwined with constitutional rights and systemic inequality.

4. Regional and Societal Identity

Words like Carolina, south, and community emphasize that regional context mattered deeply. These men weren’t just addressing national legal frameworks—they were grappling with place-based realities, especially in the American South.

  • Carolina likely surfaces in personal stories, court jurisdictions, and socio-political context.
  • The node community may reflect how each speaker emphasized public service, accountability, or the lived impact of legal victories.

5. Interconnectedness and Thematic Cohesion

The structure of the network itself—dense in certain regions, sparse in others—mirrors how certain concepts naturally cluster. For instance:

  • Law, court, judge, and case form a legal cluster.
  • Segregation, race, rights, justice form a civil rights/moral philosophy cluster.
  • School, education, university form an institutional strategy cluster.

This reveals that while the speakers used a shared vocabulary, their ideas often fell into conceptual groupings, suggesting clarity and intentionality in how they structured their narratives.

Conclusion: Who Were These Men?

This network helps us understand that Jack Greenberg, Robert L. Carter, and Matthew J. Perry were:

  • Legal architects of civil rights transformation
  • Deeply engaged with institutional and constitutional logic
  • Narratively coherent in the way they framed race, justice, and legal power
  • Focused on education and regional identity as leverage points for systemic change

This isn’t just a web of words—it’s a portrait of legal minds operating with moral vision and strategic discipline, unified by a shared language of justice and advocacy.
